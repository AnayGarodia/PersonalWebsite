Bridge

YHack 2026 · Python / Flask · Bidirectional ASL ↔ English
Built with Pratyush Agarwal at YHack 2026 — first hackathon for both of us.
↗ GitHub

Communication between Deaf and hearing people requires an interpreter — someone who exists at the intersection of two languages, two grammars, two worlds. Bridge is an attempt to automate that intersection in real time, for video calls.

The system works in two directions simultaneously: a Deaf user signing can be understood by a hearing user who sees speech, and the hearing user's speech can be understood by the Deaf user who sees signs. It integrates as a bot into Google Meet, appearing as a participant that handles all translation transparently.

System architecture

The two pipelines run concurrently over a Flask + Socket.IO backend. The ASL-to-English side processes camera frames; the English-to-ASL side processes audio. Hover each component below to see how it fits in.

ASL → English
Camera Input
OpenCV frames
Video frames captured at 30fps via OpenCV. The Google Meet bot feeds virtual camera output into this pipeline.
▼
MediaPipe
543 body landmarks
MediaPipe Holistic extracts 543 landmarks per frame — 21 per hand, 33 for pose, 468 for face. This gives rich spatial data on hand shape and position.
▼
Sign Classifier
I3D + rule-based fingerspell
An I3D model trained on WLASL100 (100 ASL signs) handles word-level recognition. A separate rule-based geometry classifier handles A–Z fingerspelling. Combined top-1 accuracy: 65.89%.
▼
Lava API
ASL → English grammar
ASL grammar differs significantly from English — it's topic-comment, not subject-verb-object. The Lava API (GPT-4o-mini backend) converts recognized sign sequences into natural English sentences.
▼
ElevenLabs TTS
Neural speech synthesis
ElevenLabs converts the English text to natural-sounding speech. Falls back to pyttsx3 offline TTS when cloud services are unavailable.
English → ASL
Microphone Input
PyAudio stream
Audio captured from the microphone via PyAudio. The Meet bot captures the hearing participant's audio track.
▼
Whisper ASR
OpenAI Whisper
OpenAI Whisper transcribes speech to text. Runs locally for low latency and privacy. Handles accents and background noise well.
▼
Lava API
English → ASL grammar
The reverse direction: converts English text to ASL gloss (a written form of ASL grammar), then maps glosses to sign identifiers from the WLASL vocabulary.
▼
SVG Avatar
Custom sign animation
A custom SVG pipeline renders a signing avatar in the browser. Each of the 100+ supported signs has a hand-authored SVG animation. Signs play sequentially at natural signing speed.

Technical notes

The sign recognition component was the hardest part. ASL is not gesture recognition — signs involve specific handshapes, movements, and locations relative to the body. The I3D model captures temporal dynamics across frames that a single-frame classifier would miss. On the WLASL100 benchmark, we achieved 65.89% top-1 accuracy, which is competitive for a 100-class problem from scratch.

The SVG avatar approach was a deliberate choice over video playback. SVG is scalable, stylable, and small. Each sign is a sequence of keyframed hand positions. The rendering engine interpolates between them and chains signs together with natural transitions. It's not perfect — a real ASL speaker would include non-manual markers (facial expression, mouth movements) that we don't render — but it's a start.

The Google Meet integration works through a virtual camera: the bot appears as a participant, renders its output to a virtual video feed using pyvirtualcam, and plays audio through a virtual audio device. This approach works without any Meet API access — it's just another participant.
