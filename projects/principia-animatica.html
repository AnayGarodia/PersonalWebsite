Mathematical objects are easy to write down and hard to see. A differential equation is a sentence about how things change, but you can stare at it for a long time without understanding what it's actually saying about the world. Animation bridges that gap — it makes the mathematics move.

Principia Animatica is a collection of mathematical animations built with Manim, the Python library Grant Sanderson built for 3Blue1Brown. Each animation is a short film: a mathematical concept rendered visually, with deliberate pacing.

Lorenz attractor

The Lorenz system is a set of three coupled ordinary differential equations, originally derived by Edward Lorenz in 1963 as a simplified model of atmospheric convection:

dx/dt = σ(y − x)
dy/dt = x(ρ − z) − y
dz/dt = xy − βz

With standard parameters (σ = 10, ρ = 28, β = 8/3), the system exhibits chaos: trajectories that start infinitesimally close together diverge exponentially. The attractor they trace has become one of the most recognizable shapes in mathematics — a double-lobed butterfly that never repeats.

The live animation below runs the system in your browser with two trajectories starting at (0.1, 0, 0) and (0.10001, 0, 0) — a difference of 0.00001 in the initial condition. Watch them diverge.

Animations

Lorenz Attractor
CODE/lorenz.py
Three trajectories from slightly different initial conditions, visualized in 3D with a rotating camera. Shows sensitivity to initial conditions and the characteristic butterfly-shaped attractor. Uses SciPy's solve_ivp for integration.
Infinity & the Product Rule
CODE/infinity.py
Animated derivation of the product rule for derivatives, with Hilbert's quote on infinity as a framing device. Dark theme with gold and indigo accents.
Animatica Suite
CODE/animatica.py
Primary animation module. A collection of shorter pieces exploring geometric series, limits, and the relationship between discrete and continuous mathematics.
