Neutronics Simulation

2025 · Python + OpenMC · Monte Carlo neutron transport
Fusion reactor breeding blanket analysis using Monte Carlo simulation
↗ GitHub

Fusion reactors don't run on uranium — they run on deuterium and tritium. Deuterium is abundant in seawater. Tritium is not. It doesn't occur naturally in significant quantities; a fusion reactor has to breed its own, using neutrons produced by the fusion reaction to transmute lithium into tritium inside a surrounding blanket. How well that blanket does this job is measured by the Tritium Breeding Ratio: the number of tritium atoms produced per fusion neutron.

This simulation uses OpenMC — a Monte Carlo neutron transport code developed at MIT — to model how neutrons from the plasma interact with different blanket materials. The central question: does replacing the conventional lithium-lead (LiPb) blanket with lithium plumbate (Li₈PbO₆) improve tritium production?

Simulation setup

The geometry is a spherical shell model — a plasma source at the center emitting 14.07 MeV neutrons (the energy produced by deuterium-tritium fusion), surrounded by concentric material layers. Each run varies the blanket thickness between 65 and 66 mm and tests four material configurations. OpenMC tracks 25,000 neutron histories per batch across 10 batches, recording tritium breeding reactions (Li-6(n,t)He-4 and Li-7(n,nt)He-4), neutron leakage fraction, and damage energy deposited in structural materials.

Damage energy is used to estimate displacements per atom (DPA) — a measure of radiation damage that limits component lifetime. A material that breeds more tritium but causes more structural damage may not be a net improvement.

Results

Li₈PbO₆ outperforms LiPb by approximately 20% on tritium breeding ratio, achieving a TBR of ~1.47 compared to ~1.22 for LiPb. The difference comes from the higher lithium atom density in the oxide compound — more lithium means more neutron capture opportunities. The trade-off is a modest increase in peak DPA in the surrounding structural steel, because the oxide matrix scatters neutrons differently than the lead-heavy LiPb alloy.

A TBR above 1.0 means the blanket produces more tritium than the reactor consumes — a necessary condition for self-sufficiency. Both materials exceed this threshold, but Li₈PbO₆'s margin is significantly larger, providing more headroom for breeding losses due to incomplete coverage and penetrations in a real reactor design.
